## 🟢 COMMON FISH

---

### 1. Sardine (*Sardina pilchardus*)

**Physical description:** Elongated and subcylindrical body, with a blue-green back and silver belly. Averages 20 cm in length, reaching up to 27 cm.

**Habitat:** Inhabits the water column (pelagic species) in coastal areas between 25 and 100 m deep. Distributed throughout the Northeast Atlantic, from Senegal to the North Sea, including the Mediterranean and Black Sea. In Portugal, it is most abundant in the north, especially between Porto and Figueira da Foz.

**Diet:** Feeds exclusively on plankton — microalgae, copepods, microscopic crustaceans, and fish eggs.

**Behavior:** Forms large schools that stay in deeper waters during the day (for protection against predators) and rise to the surface at night to feed.

**Reproduction:** Occurs between October and April, at temperatures between 14–15 °C. Each female can produce between 50,000 and 60,000 eggs per spawning.

**Lifespan:** Up to 14 years.

**Fun fact:** The sardine accumulates omega-3-rich fat from spring through fall, giving it the distinctive flavor prized at Portuguese summer festivals. It is also the main species in the Portuguese canning industry.

---

### 2. European Sea Bass (*Dicentrarchus labrax*)

**Physical description:** Elongated and compressed body with grayish tones and a pronounced dorsal profile. Can reach 1 m in length and weigh up to 11 kg.

**Habitat:** Throughout the Mediterranean, Black Sea, and Northeast Atlantic, from Norway to Senegal. Prefers shallow coastal areas, estuaries, and surf zones, occasionally entering rivers.

**Diet:** A voracious carnivorous predator. Juveniles feed on aquatic invertebrates; adults are piscivorous, also consuming crustaceans, squid, and shrimp.

**Behavior:** Young individuals form schools; adults become solitary and territorial. Very active at night and on overcast days.

**Reproduction:** Between January and March. Reaches sexual maturity around 2 years of age.

**Lifespan:** Up to 20 years.

**Fun fact:** It is a euryhaline species (tolerates large variations in salinity) and eurythermal (tolerates temperatures from 8 to 24 °C), allowing it to inhabit both the sea and estuaries, as well as the upper reaches of rivers.

---

### 3. Gilt-Head Bream (*Sparus aurata*)

**Physical description:** Oval and compressed body with a silvery color and golden reflections. Has a distinctive golden band between the eyes. Can reach 70 cm and 17 kg.

**Habitat:** Mediterranean and Eastern Atlantic. Frequents rocky and sandy bottoms, coastal lagoons, and estuaries at depths up to 150 m.

**Diet:** Carnivorous — feeds mainly on bivalves, crustaceans, sea urchins, and small fish. Its molariform teeth are adapted for crushing shells.

**Behavior:** Solitary species in adulthood. It is a protandrous hermaphrodite: born male, some individuals can become female throughout their lifetime.

**Reproduction:** Between October and December, in open water. Larvae and juveniles migrate to shallow estuarine areas.

**Fun fact:** It is one of the most farmed species in aquaculture in Portugal and throughout Mediterranean Europe. Its golden band gave it its name.

---

### 4. Atlantic Chub Mackerel (*Scomber colias*)

**Physical description:** Fusiform and streamlined body, blue-green on the back with dark stripes and a silver belly. Reaches 50 cm in length.

**Habitat:** Northeast Atlantic and Mediterranean, in coastal and oceanic waters. A pelagic species, very common along the Portuguese coast.

**Diet:** Opportunistic — feeds on zooplankton, small fish, and cephalopods.

**Behavior:** Forms large migratory schools. A very agile and fast species.

**Reproduction:** From March to June. Spawns in open water in warm conditions.

**Fun fact:** The chub mackerel is one of the main pelagic species landed at Portuguese ports and holds great economic importance. Its flesh is rich in healthy fats (omega-3).

---

### 5. Tub Gurnard (*Trigla lucerna*)

**Physical description:** Robust body with a large, flattened head covered in bony plates. Reddish to orange coloration on the back. The pectoral fins have free rays that function as "legs." Can reach 75 cm.

**Habitat:** Northeast Atlantic and Mediterranean. Lives on sandy and muddy bottoms between 20 and 300 m deep.

**Diet:** Benthic carnivore — feeds on crustaceans, worms, mollusks, and small fish found on the seafloor.

**Behavior:** Uses its modified pectoral fins to "walk" along the bottom and detect buried prey. Produces sounds through its swim bladder (one of the few fish that "grunts").

**Fun fact:** Its English name, *tub gurnard*, comes from the sound it produces. The species has firm and flavorful flesh, highly regarded in Mediterranean and Portuguese cuisine.

## 🔵 RARE FISH

---

### 6. Ocean Sunfish (*Mola mola*)

**Physical description:** The largest bony fish in the world. Almost circular body, laterally flattened, with no true caudal fin — replaced by a "clavus" (a wavy structure). Rough, scaleless skin. Can reach 4 m in height and weigh over 2,300 kg.

**Habitat:** A cosmopolitan species, found in the Atlantic, Pacific, Indian Oceans, and the Mediterranean, in temperate and tropical waters. Inhabits the epipelagic zone (up to 200 m), but regularly dives to depths greater than 600 m to feed.

**Diet:** Primarily jellyfish, salps, and gelatinous zooplankton. Also consumes small fish, crustaceans, and squid. Its diet is low in nutrients, requiring it to eat in large quantities.

**Behavior:** Frequently seen "sunbathing" at the surface — behavior associated with the need to warm up after deep dives into cold water. When swimming near the surface, its fin causes it to be mistaken for a shark.

**Reproduction:** The female is the most fertile known vertebrate: she can release up to 300 million eggs in a single spawning.

**Fun fact:** It can host up to 60 different species of parasites on its skin and internal organs. It has only 16 vertebrae — the fewest of any fish.

---

### 7. Garfish (*Belone belone*)

**Physical description:** Extremely elongated and slender body, with a beak-shaped mouth full of sharp teeth. Blue-green coloration on the back and silver on the belly. Reaches 90 cm in length.

**Habitat:** Northeast Atlantic, Mediterranean, and Black Sea. A pelagic species that prefers shallow coastal waters.

**Diet:** Active predator — hunts small fish at the surface.

**Behavior:** Forms schools and is capable of leaping out of the water when disturbed or fleeing.

**Fun fact:** The garfish's bones have a distinctive blue-green color, caused by biliverdin. This characteristic alarmed some people in the past, but it is completely normal and the flesh is safe to eat.

---

### 8. Black Scabbardfish (*Aphanopus carbo*)

**Physical description:** Very elongated, flattened, and dark silver body with metallic reflections. Can exceed 2 m in length. No visible scales. Large, sharp teeth.

**Habitat:** North and East Atlantic, at depths between 200 and 1,700 m. In Madeira and the Azores, it is a species of great economic importance.

**Diet:** Deep-sea predator — feeds on octopuses, squid, crustaceans, and other fish.

**Fun fact:** It is a very important species in Madeiran cuisine. Fishing is done at night using deep longlines. Madeira is one of the only places in the world where it is regularly consumed as a traditional dish.

---

### 9. Oceanic Puffer Fish (*Lagocephalus lagocephalus*)

**Physical description:** Oval and stocky body, capable of inflating with water or air when threatened. Bluish back and white belly. Can reach 60 cm.

**Habitat:** Atlantic, Indian, and Pacific Oceans, in tropical and temperate waters. Occasionally spotted along the Portuguese coast.

**Diet:** Carnivorous — feeds on mollusks, crustaceans, sea urchins, and other invertebrates, which it crushes with its fused beak-like teeth.

**Toxicity:** Contains tetrodotoxin (TTX), one of the most potent known neurotoxins, concentrated mainly in the liver, roe, and skin. Consumption without proper preparation can be fatal.

**Fun fact:** It belongs to the same order as the puffer fish used in Japanese cuisine (*fugu*), which can only be prepared by licensed chefs. In Portugal, its consumption is strongly discouraged.

---

### 10. Rabbit Fish / Ratfish (*Chimaera monstrosa*)

**Physical description:** Ghostly appearance — large head, pointed snout, large eyes with a silver-green color, body tapering to a filamentous tail. Can reach 1.5 m. A distant relative of sharks (cartilaginous), but with unique characteristics.

**Habitat:** Northeast Atlantic and Mediterranean, at depths between 200 and 1,000 m. Inhabits rocky and muddy bottoms.

**Diet:** Benthic — feeds on crustaceans, echinoderms, mollusks, and small fish found on the seafloor.

**Fun fact:** It has a venomous spine on its dorsal fin as a defense mechanism. The name "chimaera" comes from the Greek mythological creature — a being composed of parts of different animals, just as its hybrid appearance suggests.

## 🔴 ENDANGERED FISH

---

### 11. Atlantic Sturgeon (*Acipenser sturio*)

**Physical description:** A large fish with a serpentine body, long flattened snout, toothless inferior mouth, and four barbels. The body is covered with bony plates instead of scales. Can reach 3 m in length and weigh up to 300 kg.

**Habitat:** Northeast Atlantic and rivers of Western Europe. It was an anadromous species — it lived in the sea and migrated to rivers to spawn. In Portugal, it inhabited the Douro, Tagus, and Guadiana rivers.

**Diet:** Mollusks, crustaceans, and small fish, detected using its sensitive barbels.

**Conservation status:** Critically Endangered (IUCN). In Portugal it has been **extinct in the wild** since the 1980s. The last specimens were caught in the Guadiana River.

**Causes of decline:** Overfishing (especially during reproductive migration), construction of dams that blocked migration routes, river pollution, and sediment extraction.

**Fun fact:** It is considered a "living fossil" — its lineage has existed for over 100 million years. In Mértola, it was depicted on coins minted in the 1st century BC. The only viable European population currently survives in the Gironde River in France.

---

### 12. Undulate Ray (*Raja undulata*)

**Physical description:** Flattened diamond-shaped body, brownish in color with orange spots on the back and a white belly. Can reach 1.2 m in length.

**Habitat:** Northeast Atlantic and Mediterranean, on sandy and muddy bottoms up to 200 m deep. Present along the Portuguese coast.

**Diet:** Benthic — feeds on crustaceans, mollusks, and small fish.

**Conservation status:** Critically Endangered (IUCN). Listed in the Red Book of Vertebrates of Portugal.

**Causes of decline:** Accidental capture in fishing gear (bycatch), destruction of benthic habitats, and a low reproductive rate (few eggs per year).

**Fun fact:** Rays are cartilaginous, like sharks, with whom they share common ancestors. They lay eggs in rigid capsules known as "mermaid's purses."

---

### 13. Dusky Grouper (*Epinephelus marginatus*)

**Physical description:** A robust and bulky fish with yellowish-brown coloration and irregular darker spots. Can reach 1.5 m in length and weigh over 60 kg.

**Habitat:** Mediterranean and Eastern Atlantic, from the British Isles to South Africa. Inhabits rocky reefs and caves up to 300 m deep.

**Diet:** A solitary apex predator — feeds on octopuses, lobsters, crabs, and fish.

**Conservation status:** Endangered (IUCN). Heavily threatened by spearfishing and commercial fishing.

**Behavior:** A territorial and curious species with low mobility. It is a protogynous hermaphrodite: born female, it may become male with age.

**Fun fact:** It can live for over 50 years. Its low reproductive rate and habitat fidelity make recovery very slow after overexploitation.

---

### 14. Cuckoo Wrasse (*Labrus mixtus*)

**Physical description:** Marked sexual dimorphism — males are blue-green with orange markings; females are reddish with white spots. Can reach 40 cm.

**Habitat:** Northeast Atlantic, from northwest Africa to Norway, including the Mediterranean. Inhabits rocky bottoms and algae beds up to 200 m deep.

**Diet:** Crustaceans, echinoderms, mollusks, and worms.

**Conservation status:** Vulnerable (IUCN).

**Fun fact:** Like many wrasses, it is a protogynous hermaphrodite (born female, able to transform into male). It is known for "cleaning" other fish of parasites, functioning as natural cleaning stations.

---

### 15. Smalltooth Sawfish (*Pristis pectinata*)

**Physical description:** One of the most impressive animals in the ocean — shark-shaped body with a long, flattened, pointed rostrum covered in lateral teeth (like a saw). Can reach 7 m in length.

**Habitat:** Subtropical Atlantic, Mediterranean Sea (practically extinct), and tropical coastal areas. Frequents estuaries and shallow coastal zones, and can enter rivers.

**Diet:** Uses its rostrum as an electrical sensor and weapon to stun fish.

**Conservation status:** Critically Endangered (IUCN). Considered functionally extinct in the Mediterranean.

**Causes of decline:** The rostrum easily gets entangled in fishing nets; destruction of coastal and estuarine habitats; historical direct fishing.

**Fun fact:** Despite its appearance, it is a ray (not a shark). Its rostrum does not consist of true teeth, but rather projections of the skull cartilage.

## ⚫ EXTINCT FISH

---

### 16. Chinese Paddlefish (*Psephurus gladius*)

**Physical description:** One of the largest freshwater fish in the world. Elongated body with a sword-shaped (paddle-like) rostrum that could represent a third of its total length. Leathery skin without scales. Could reach 7 m in length and weigh up to 450 kg.

**Habitat:** Endemic to the Yangtze River, the largest river in Asia, in China.

**Diet:** Active predator — used its rostrum as an electromagnetic wave detector to locate prey (medium-sized fish).

**Date of extinction:** The last living specimen was sighted in 2003. Officially declared extinct on July 21, 2022.

**Causes of extinction:** Intensive overfishing (up to 25 tons per year in the 1970s), construction of the Gezhouba and Three Gorges dams (which divided the population and prevented migration), river pollution, and habitat destruction.

**Fun fact:** Its lineage has existed for about 200 million years — it survived the extinction of the dinosaurs. It was nicknamed "the panda of the Yangtze River" and "the king of freshwater fish." It is the first large freshwater fish to go extinct in the modern era.

---

### 17. Smooth Handfish (*Sympterichthys unipennis*)

**Physical description:** A small benthic fish of about 15 cm. Unusual appearance — bulging eyes, a crest-like dorsal fin (resembling a mohawk), and pectoral and pelvic fins modified into "hand"-like structures it used to "walk" along the seafloor.

**Habitat:** Endemic to the shallow coastal waters of southeastern Tasmania (Australia), at shallow depths.

**Diet:** Used a lure-like organ (*illicium*) on its head, similar to that of anglerfish, to attract prey.

**Date of extinction:** Declared extinct by the IUCN in 2020 — the **first marine bony fish to be declared extinct in modern times**.

**Causes of extinction:** The only known specimen was caught in 1802 by French naturalist François Péron. The species was wiped out by intensive scallop and oyster fishing (which destroyed the seafloor where it lived), rising water temperatures, and coastal pollution near Tasmania. It had no free larval stage — juveniles hatched from eggs already as miniature versions of the adults, making them extremely vulnerable to local disturbances.

**Fun fact:** The only known specimen is preserved at the National Museum of Natural History in Paris, where it has been since 1802. The species was described as "peculiar and charismatic."

---

### 18. Galapagos Damsel (*Azurina eupalama*)

**Physical description:** A small fish of about 15 cm, with a slender, fusiform body. Olive-gray coloration with blue and silver tones on the belly. A distinctive black spot at the base of the pectoral fins.

**Habitat:** Endemic to the Galápagos Islands (Ecuador) and possibly the Cocos Islands. Lived on rocky reefs and open waters between 5 and 30 m deep.

**Diet:** Zooplankton, crustacean larvae, and small invertebrates.

**Date of extinction:** Disappeared during the severe El Niño of 1982–83 and has not been observed since. Considered probably extinct.

**Causes of extinction:** The 1982–83 El Niño caused a drastic rise in water temperatures in the Eastern Pacific, which destroyed much of the archipelago's plankton — the dietary foundation of this species. Before this event, it was a common fish easily spotted by divers in the Galápagos.

**Fun fact:** The name *Azurina* comes from the Latin for "pale blue," referring to its coloration. It was a sociable species that formed large schools in coastal waters — a characteristic that would make it easy to spot if it still existed, which led scientists to conclude it is very likely extinct.

### 19. Mauritian Wrasse (*Symphodus melanocercus*)

> ⚠️ *Note: The "Mauritian Green Wrasse" is a common name that may refer to species of the genus Cheilinus or Labridae that are endemic or regional to the Mascarene Islands. Some species in this group are extinct or critically endangered due to coral reef destruction and overfishing.*

**Description:** A colorful wrasse with an oval body and vivid colors (greens, blues, and oranges), typical of tropical coral reefs in the Indian Ocean.

**Habitat:** Coral reefs of the Mascarene Islands and the tropical Indian Ocean.

**Diet:** Benthic invertebrates — crustaceans, mollusks, and echinoderms.

**Status:** Tropical wrasses from the Mascarene Islands have suffered severe population decline due to coral reef destruction from bleaching (rising water temperatures) and overfishing.

**Fun fact:** Wrasses are protogynous — all offspring are born female; larger, dominant males are females that have changed sex. This characteristic makes them especially vulnerable to selective overfishing of the largest individuals.

### 20. Dunkleosteus (*Dunkleosteus terrelli*)

**Physical description:** One of the most formidable predators in the history of life on Earth. It belonged to the class of **placoderms** — armored fish with bony shields covering the head and thorax. Instead of teeth, it had sharp bony plates that functioned like giant scissors. The body was likely cartilaginous (non-fossilizable), making size estimates difficult. Recent estimates suggest about 4 to 8 m in length, with weight potentially reaching 1 ton.

**Habitat:** Shallow coastal seas worldwide during the **Devonian Period** (between 380 and 360 million years ago). Fossils found in the USA, Canada, Poland, Belgium, and Morocco.

**Diet:** Hypercarnivorous apex predator — it hunted fish, primitive sharks, cephalopods, and other placoderms. There is evidence of cannibalism. Its bite was extremely powerful — estimated at 7,400 Newtons.

**Extinction:** It went extinct at the end of the Devonian, about 360 million years ago, during a mass extinction event that eliminated 75% of marine species.

**Fun fact:** Dunkleosteus was named in 1956 in honor of David Dunkle, curator of the Cleveland Museum of Natural History (USA), where some of the finest fossil specimens are held. Despite its size and fearsome appearance, it is believed to have been a relatively slow swimmer that hunted by ambush. Since only the bony head plates were preserved, the reconstruction of its body remains a subject of scientific debate.
