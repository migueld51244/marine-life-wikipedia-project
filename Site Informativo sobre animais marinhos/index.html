Sardine (Sardina pilchardus)

Physical description:

Elongated and subcylindrical body, with a blue-green back and silver belly. Averages 20 cm in length, reaching up to 27 cm.

Habitat:

Inhabits the water column (pelagic species) in coastal areas between 25 and 100 m deep. Distributed throughout the Northeast Atlantic, from Senegal to the North Sea, including the Mediterranean and Black Sea. In Portugal, it is most abundant in the north, especially between Porto and Figueira da Foz.

Diet:

Feeds exclusively on plankton — microalgae, copepods, microscopic crustaceans, and fish eggs.

Behavior:

Forms large schools that stay in deeper waters during the day (for protection against predators) and rise to the surface at night to feed.

Reproduction:

Occurs between October and April, at temperatures between 14 to 15 °C. Each female can produce between 50,000 and 60,000 eggs per spawning.

Lifespan:

Up to 14 years.

Fun fact:

The sardine accumulates omega-3-rich fat from spring through fall, giving it the distinctive flavor prized at Portuguese summer festivals. It is also the main species in the Portuguese canning industry.

European Sea Bass (Dicentrarchus labrax)

Physical description:

Elongated and compressed body with grayish tones and a pronounced dorsal profile. Can reach 1 m in length and weigh up to 11 kg.

Habitat:

Throughout the Mediterranean, Black Sea, and Northeast Atlantic, from Norway to Senegal. Prefers shallow coastal areas, estuaries, and surf zones, occasionally entering rivers.

Diet:

A voracious carnivorous predator. Juveniles feed on aquatic invertebrates; adults are piscivorous, also consuming crustaceans, squid, and shrimp.

Behavior:

Young individuals form schools; adults become solitary and territorial. Very active at night and on overcast days.

Reproduction:

Between January and March. Reaches sexual maturity around 2 years of age.

Lifespan:

Up to 20 years.

Fun fact:

It is a euryhaline species (tolerates large variations in salinity) and eurythermal (tolerates temperatures from 8 to 24 °C), allowing it to inhabit both the sea and estuaries, as well as the upper reaches of rivers.

Atlantic Chub Mackerel (Scomber colias)

Physical description:

Fusiform and streamlined body, blue-green on the back with dark stripes and a silver belly. Reaches 50 cm in length.

Habitat:

Northeast Atlantic and Mediterranean, in coastal and oceanic waters. A pelagic species, very common along the Portuguese coast.

Diet:

Opportunistic — feeds on zooplankton, small fish, and cephalopods.

Behavior:

Forms large migratory schools. A very agile and fast species.

Reproduction:

From March to June. Spawns in open water in warm conditions.

Lifespan:

5 to 13 years.

Fun fact:

The chub mackerel is one of the main pelagic species landed at Portuguese ports and holds great economic importance. Its flesh is rich in healthy fats (omega-3).

Gilt-Head Bream (Sparus aurata)

Physical description:

Oval and compressed body with a silvery color and golden reflections. Has a distinctive golden band between the eyes. Can reach 70 cm and 17 kg.

Habitat:

Mediterranean and Eastern Atlantic. Frequents rocky and sandy bottoms, coastal lagoons, and estuaries at depths up to 150 m.

Diet:

Carnivorous — feeds mainly on bivalves, crustaceans, sea urchins, and small fish. Its molariform teeth are adapted for crushing shells.

Behavior:

Solitary species in adulthood. It is a protandrous hermaphrodite: born male, some individuals can become female throughout their lifetime.

Reproduction:

Between October and December, in open water. Larvae and juveniles migrate to shallow estuarine areas.

Lifespan:

Up to 11 years.

Fun fact:

It is one of the most farmed species in aquaculture in Portugal and throughout Mediterranean Europe. Its golden band gave it its name.

Tub Gurnard (Trigla lucerna)

Physical description:

Robust body with a large, flattened head covered in bony plates. Reddish to orange coloration on the back. The pectoral fins have free rays that function as "legs." Can reach 75 cm.

Habitat:

Northeast Atlantic and Mediterranean. Lives on sandy and muddy bottoms between 20 and 300 m deep.

Diet:

Benthic carnivore — feeds on crustaceans, worms, mollusks, and small fish found on the seafloor.

Behavior:

Uses its modified pectoral fins to "walk" along the bottom and detect buried prey. Produces sounds through its swim bladder (one of the few fish that "grunts").

Reproduction:

They reproduce by releasing eggs into open water (pelagic spawning) during a breeding season that generally peaks between December and May, though it can extend throughout the year in some regions. Females can lay approximately 150,000 eggs, with sexual maturity reached around age 1–2 (roughly 15–20 cm)

Lifespan:

Approximately 15 years.

Fun fact:

Its English name, tub gurnard, comes from the sound it produces. The species has firm and flavorful flesh, highly regarded in Mediterranean and Portuguese cuisine.

Ocean Sunfish (Mola mola)

Physical description:

The largest bony fish in the world. Almost circular body, laterally flattened, with no true caudal fin — replaced by a "clavus" (a wavy structure). Rough, scaleless skin. Can reach 4 m in height and weigh over 2,300 kg.

Habitat:

A cosmopolitan species, found in the Atlantic, Pacific, Indian Oceans, and the Mediterranean, in temperate and tropical waters. Inhabits the epipelagic zone (up to 200 m), but regularly dives to depths greater than 600 m to feed.

Diet:

Primarily jellyfish, salps, and gelatinous zooplankton. Also consumes small fish, crustaceans, and squid. Its diet is low in nutrients, requiring it to eat in large quantities.

Behavior:

Frequently seen "sunbathing" at the surface — behavior associated with the need to warm up after deep dives into cold water. When swimming near the surface, its fin causes it to be mistaken for a shark.

Reproduction:

The female is the most fertile known vertebrate: she can release up to 300 million eggs in a single spawning.

Lifespan:

16–23 years or potentially longer

Fun fact:

It can host up to 60 different species of parasites on its skin and internal organs. It has only 16 vertebrae — the fewest of any fish.

Garfish (Belone belone)

Physical description:

Extremely elongated and slender body, with a beak-shaped mouth full of sharp teeth. Blue-green coloration on the back and silver on the belly. Reaches 90 cm in length.

Habitat:

Northeast Atlantic, Mediterranean, and Black Sea. A pelagic species that prefers shallow coastal waters.

Diet:

Active predator — hunts small fish at the surface.

Behavior:

Forms schools and is capable of leaping out of the water when disturbed or fleeing.

Reproduction:

Spawning typically occurs in spring and early summer, with variations depending on region: March to May/June in the Mediterranean, and May to September for the Black Sea subspecies (Belone euxini). They prefer shallow, sandy shores, often spawning on seagrass beds (Zostera noltii).

Lifespan:

Up to 10-18 years

Fun fact:

The garfish's bones have a distinctive blue-green color, caused by biliverdin. This characteristic alarmed some people in the past, but it is completely normal and the flesh is safe to eat.

Black Scabbardfish (Aphanopus carbo)

Physical description:

Very elongated, flattened, and dark silver body with metallic reflections. Can exceed 2 m in length. No visible scales. Large, sharp teeth.

Habitat:

North and East Atlantic, at depths between 200 and 1,700 m. In Madeira and the Azores, it is a species of great economic importance.

Diet:

Deep-sea predator — feeds on octopuses, squid, crustaceans, and other fish.

Behavior:

Reproduction:

Black scabbardfish (Aphanopus carbo) are deep-water, iteroparous total spawners with determinate fecundity, primarily spawning near the Madeira and Canary Islands between September and December. They exhibit large-scale migratory behavior in the Northeast Atlantic, with mature adults moving south to spawn, while younger, immature fish are found further north, notably off the British Isles.

Lifespan:

12 to 14 years

Fun fact:

It is a very important species in Madeiran cuisine. Fishing is done at night using deep longlines. Madeira is one of the only places in the world where it is regularly consumed as a traditional dish.

Ocean Puffer Fish (Lagocephalus lagocephalus)

Physical description:

Oval and stocky body, capable of inflating with water or air when threatened. Bluish back and white belly. Can reach 60 cm.

Habitat:

Atlantic, Indian, and Pacific Oceans, in tropical and temperate waters. Occasionally spotted along the Portuguese coast.

Diet:

Carnivorous — feeds on mollusks, crustaceans, sea urchins, and other invertebrates, which it crushes with its fused beak-like teeth.

Behavior:

It is known for its ability to inflate when threatened, its opportunistic hunting behavior, and its powerful, beak-like teeth.

Reproduction:

An oviparous pelagic fish that releases buoyant, spherical eggs into the open ocean. Spawning typically occurs in warmer, temperate to subtropical waters, with evidence of maturity found in males during late winter (e.g., December). The species is known for a rapid, high-investment reproductive strategy.

Lifespan:

Upwards to 10 years in the wild.

Fun fact:

It belongs to the same order as the puffer fish used in Japanese cuisine (*fugu*), which can only be prepared by licensed chefs. In Portugal, its consumption is strongly discouraged.

Toxicity:

Contains tetrodotoxin (TTX), one of the most potent known neurotoxins, concentrated mainly in the liver, roe, and skin. Consumption without proper preparation can be fatal.

Rabbit Fish / Ratfish (Chimaera monstrosa)

Physical description:

Ghostly appearance — large head, pointed snout, large eyes with a silver-green color, body tapering to a filamentous tail. Can reach 1.5 m. A distant relative of sharks (cartilaginous), but with unique characteristics.

Habitat:

Northeast Atlantic and Mediterranean, at depths between 200 and 1,000 m. Inhabits rocky and muddy bottoms.

Diet:

Benthic — feeds on crustaceans, echinoderms, mollusks, and small fish found on the seafloor.

Behavior:

They are sluggish benthic feeders that swim by waving pectoral fins, consuming invertebrates like crabs. They demonstrate sexual segregation by depth and are known for, a mildly venomous dorsal spine.

Reproduction:

Reproduces via internal fertilization and is oviparous, with females laying pairs of long,, slender, leathery egg cases on the seabed. Males use a unique, retractable, tentaculum (club-like organ) on their head with sharp denticles to grasp the female during copulation.

Lifespan:

Up to 30 years.

Fun fact:

It has a venomous spine on its dorsal fin as a defense mechanism. The name "chimaera" comes from the Greek mythological creature — a being composed of parts of different animals, just as its hybrid appearance suggests.

Atlantic Sturgeon (Acipenser sturio)

Physical description:

A large fish with a serpentine body, long flattened snout, toothless inferior mouth, and four barbels. The body is covered with bony plates instead of scales. Can reach 3 m in length and weigh up to 300 kg.

Habitat:

Northeast Atlantic and rivers of Western Europe. It was an anadromous species — it lived in the sea and migrated to rivers to spawn. In Portugal, it inhabited the Douro, Tagus, and Guadiana rivers.

Diet:

Mollusks, crustaceans, and small fish, detected using its sensitive barbels.

Behavior:

The behavioral patterns of the Atlantic sturgeon (Acipenser sturio) are shaped by their ancient biology and deep-water lifestyle. Because they live near the bottom of murky rivers and coastal seas, their behavior focuses heavily on sensory navigation, seasonal movement, and energy conservation.

Reproduction:

Reproduction in the Atlantic sturgeon (Acipenser sturio) is an intense, high-energy event characterized by extreme late maturity, precise environmental triggers, and frantic group spawning.Because they are anadromous, they completely shift their behavior and physiology to transition from the ocean back into freshwater rivers solely to pass on their genes.

Conservation Status:

Critically Endangered (IUCN). In Portugal it has been extinct in the wild since the 1980s. The last specimens were caught in the Guadiana River.

Causes of decline:

Overfishing (especially during reproductive migration), construction of dams that blocked migration routes, river pollution, and sediment extraction.

Lifespan:

Up to 60 years or more.

Fun fact:

It is considered a "living fossil" — its lineage has existed for over 100 million years. In Mértola, it was depicted on coins minted in the 1st century BC. The only viable European population currently survives in the Gironde River in France.

Undulate Ray (Raja undulata)

Physical description:

Flattened diamond-shaped body, brownish in color with orange spots on the back and a white belly. Can reach 1.2 m in length.

Habitat:

Northeast Atlantic and Mediterranean, on sandy and muddy bottoms up to 200 m deep. Present along the Portuguese coast.

Diet:

Benthic — feeds on crustaceans, mollusks, and small fish.

Behavior:

This species, which is often considered to be a skate, is known for its distinct, wavy, dark-banded pattern and sedentary, solitary nature.

Reproduction:

Oviparous (egg-laying) elasmobranch that reproduces in winter/spring, laying approximately 30–90 leathery, 7–9 cm egg capsules—known as "mermaid's purses"—on sandy or muddy seabeds. These rays display slow growth and late maturity (around 7–9 years), which makes them highly susceptible to overexploitation and contributes to their threatened status.
